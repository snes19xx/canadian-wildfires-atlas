2023 doubled the record

Canadian fire agencies report every wildfire that grows past 200 hectares to a national database. The record runs from 1959 to 2025 and holds 20,946 large fires, together covering about 160 million hectares. 2023 sits far outside the rest of it: 17.6 million hectares burned in one season, more than twice the previous record. 2025 is now the second-worst year, and 2024 also made the top seven, so three of the seven worst years on record are the last three.

Bigger fires, not more of them

The number of large fires per decade has stayed near three thousand for sixty years. The change is in size: a large fire in 2016–2025 averaged 10,397 hectares, 1.9 times the average of 1960–1980. Growth, not ignition, is what drives the record years.

Lightning burns the most land

People start fires close to roads and towns, where suppression reaches them early. Lightning starts them in remote forest, where it can take days for crews to arrive, if they arrive at all. 16,318 of the 20,946 large fires were natural ignitions, and they account for 86% of the total area burned.

The Geographic Distribution

Most of the burning happens in a belt across the western boreal forest: the Northwest Territories and the northern parts of Alberta, Saskatchewan and Manitoba, with a second concentration in Quebec. The map layers above show this in different ways: density is total burned area over the full record, hotspots marks the statistically significant clusters, trend shows where annual burned area is rising, and reburns shows land that has burned more than once since 1972.

The largest fires on record

Burning is concentrated in a small number of very large fires. The 243 fires that passed 100,000 hectares are about one percent of the record and roughly a third of the area burned. Hover a row to see the fire's burn scar on the map above.

2023, day by day

The 2023 button above the map replays the season from satellite data: 1.6 million VIIRS thermal detections over the year. Quebec's fires start with a burst of lightning in early June; the west and the territories burn from May into October.

Method

Timeline and figures: NFDB large-fire points (≥ 200 ha), 1959–2025, cleaned of invalid years and prescribed burns. Annual area burned is cross-checked against NBAC adjusted hectares (1972–2025); the two differ by source and method (for 2023, 17.6 Mha NFDB vs 14.8 Mha NBAC).

Density: fires are binned to a 5 km grid weighted by burned area, and the grid \(A\) is convolved with a Gaussian kernel of σ = 25 km. Values are log-scaled, with cells under 50 ha left blank, and drawn as six classed contour bands:

$$ D = A * G_{\sigma}, \qquad v = \left(\frac{\ln(1+D)-\ln(1+50)}{\ln(1+D_{\max})-\ln(1+50)}\right)^{\!1.6} $$

Hotspots: Getis–Ord \(G_i^{*}\) on total burned area per 25 km cell, with binary KNN-8 weights and analytic inference, shown from z ≥ 1.65:

$$ G_i^{*} = \frac{\sum_j w_{ij}x_j - \bar{x}\sum_j w_{ij}} {S\sqrt{\bigl[\,n\sum_j w_{ij}^{2} - \bigl(\sum_j w_{ij}\bigr)^{2}\bigr]/(n-1)}} $$

Trend: the Theil–Sen slope of decadal mean burned area \(d\) per 50 km cell, masked where the Mann–Kendall statistic \(S\) on the annual series gives p > 0.1:

$$ \beta = \operatorname*{median}_{i<j}\,\frac{d_j - d_i}{t_j - t_i}, \qquad S = \sum_{i<j}\operatorname{sgn}(x_j - x_i) $$

Reburns: NBAC fire polygons rasterized at 2 km and summed, so each cell counts how many times it has burned since 1972. Severity: CanLaBS Landsat burn severity (1985–2024), the normalized burn ratio differenced across each fire and classed at 270 and 660:

$$ \mathrm{NBR} = \frac{\rho_{\text{NIR}}-\rho_{\text{SWIR2}}}{\rho_{\text{NIR}}+\rho_{\text{SWIR2}}}, \qquad \mathrm{dNBR} = 1000\,(\mathrm{NBR}_{\text{pre}} - \mathrm{NBR}_{\text{post}}) $$

2023 playback: FIRMS VIIRS SNPP C2 detections, vegetation fires at nominal-or-better confidence, counted per day on a 6 km grid; a cell with \(n\) detections draws with magnitude \(\min\bigl(1, \tfrac{1}{2.5}\log_{10}(1+n)\bigr)\).

Data: Canadian National Fire Database (NFDB) point data & National Burned Area Composite (NBAC), CWFIS / Canadian Forest Service, Natural Resources Canada · CanLaBS Landsat burn severity · FIRMS VIIRS active fire data, NASA LANCE/FIRMS · basemap Natural Earth.
